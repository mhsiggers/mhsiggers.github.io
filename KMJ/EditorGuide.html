As editor of a paper your tasks are:
  • Triage. (Deciding which papers should be rejected editorially (without extermal review), and which should be send onto reviewers.)
  • Finding Reviewers.
  • Processing the paper through revision.

Accepting/Declining the assignment

Assigments are based mostly on the first 2 digits of the MSC numbers, so which can be quite a broad classification. If you are completely unable to assess a paper, please refuse the assignment, (rather than leaving the invitation outstanding). Keep in mind though, that if you refuse it, it will often fall to me to assess it, and I am likely further from the area than you are. So a comment like "Not really my area, but looks weak." is actually quite useful.

Ideally I try not to send any editor more thatn 10-15 assignments per year. Some editors take more and some say this is too much. You are welcome suggets a number that fits you better. If you are getting too many assignments, please let me know. ( I can loose track of how much you are doing, so set your boundaries. I would rather you do less than to quit outright.).

Triage

The KMJ presently gets about 400 submissions per year and publishes 60. So we must reject about %85 of our submissions. To save us work, and to speed up the processing for the authors, we would like to editorially reject about %70 of papers. About %50 of papers are obvious rejects, and I take care of that, but the other decisions come down to the editors. This is the task for which you are the most valuable, as to make a judgement about which papers are the top 15% - 25%, one needs to be somewhat close to the subject area.

To reject a paper editorially, select "Reject without external review" on the Paper Detail screen. Some small justification is nice, but is not necessary. (Especially if the paper is recent -- its a little unfair to reject papers older than a month or two without some reason to the authors.) A comment such as the following suffices. (If you give a reason, I usually append this comment from the editor-in-chief.)

We regret to inform you that after careful consideration of your submission, the editorial board of the Kyungpook Mathematical Journal has decided that this paper is not suitable for publication in the KMJ at this time. Our decision is based on the interest of this article to our editorial board and to our general readership. Due to a very high volume of submissions our acceptance rate is quite low. We have to select the best of the submissions we receive.

How to triage

It may be difficult to judge what the top %15-%25 of submissions is. Some other rules-of-thumb criterea are that acceptable papers should ...

Be at at least a lower SCIE level.

We are an ESCI journal and hoping to get onto the SCIE list soon. If you don't have a good feel for what the top 15% of our submissions looks like, think that we should be looking to publish only papers that are strong enough to get into the (lower) SCIE journals.

Have wide (international) appeal.

  • If a paper seems only interesting to a pocket of authors localised in one or two countries, it should be suggested that a more specialised journal would be more appropriate. "Though this result seems interesting to a specialised audience, it is not appropriate to a journal, such as the KMJ, of general scope. Perhaps the authors should consider a more specialised journal, such as ... "
  • If all the references are either self references or references to papers more than thirty years old, the result is probably too specific for the KMJ.

Be well written.

Most of our authors are not native English speakers, so we try not to reject solely on poor English or writing. However, there is a point where the writing makes the paper unclear, and such papers become difficult to handle. Poorly written papers are difficult to get reviewers for, and use up a lot of editorial resources. If the English seems a lot of work, please only accept the paper if is seems otherwise very strong. Do not, however, worry about correcting the English. We will handle this later, either by sending it back to the author for English revision, or getting it edited.

Finding reviewers

This tends to be the most difficult task.

We would like to have at least two reviewers for a paper, but we will accept or reject on only one review. Often, however, reviewers don't follow through, or give useless reviews (see Reviewer Guidelines), and so we have to get further reviews. It is useful therefore to try to get more reviewers from the start.

When soliciting potential reviewers we do not know, one often has to make 6 - 8 invitations. With some practice, the hit rate gets better, but never great. I generally invite 3-4 people first off.

If you have trouble finding reviewers, it is tempting to just review the manuscript yourself. This can become a big burden though. So I try to keep it as a last resort for papers that are getting too long overdue without a review. Before this we suggest you rather ask us for help in finding a reviewer. (Unlike for Triage, we can do this with little knowledge of the area.) We have some tips for finding reviewers below.

  • By the nature of the submissions we get, we have found that looking to the references for reviewers is not always a good idea. Our submissions often come from authors in a publishing 'pod' who cross reference each other, and so give very superficial reviews with an 'accept' decision.
  • If you know someone good in the area, this is nice, but be careful not to go to often to the same pool.
  • Use MathSciNet. Doing a search for by MSC number and/or keywords is always a good first step. Sometimes the MSC number is obsolete, this suggests that the area is not currently of interest to researchers, (but this is not a sure thing.) If many recent results come up, I first try to find recent ones in good journals. Among these I try to find researchers from a different country than the authors, who have several publications, and who have not collaborated (much) with the authors. Clicking on researchers names, you can find a researcher's collaborators and publications. It is good to check that the researcher has several publications in the same general area.
  • Even if the paper is from a publishing pod, it is usually addressing some old paper that appeared in a top journal. It is often useful to look up this paper on MathSciNet. From there you can find recent papers that reference it. This can get you out of the pod, but to people that know the area.
  • If one is having trouble finding reviewers, one can ask the author for suggestions. I often use a message as follows if I have not been able to find reviewers.
    We are sorry for the long delay in processing your paper. Unfortunately we are having considerable trouble finding reviewers for the manuscript. Invitations have gone out to 12 reviewers, and none of them have accepted our invitation. (Two did, but their reviews are long overdue, and they do not respond to our enquiries.) This suggests that the audience for the paper might be too specialized, and it is at this time that we start re-evaluating the paper for editorial rejection.

    As we would hate to reject the paper without a review after such a long wait, we would like to give you an opportunity to suggest some reviewers. If you could suggest reviewers that you know to be competent and familiar with the subject, we will consider sending the paper to them. As we would like to keep the reviewing process anonymous, we ask that you suggest several. Please also disclose any close relationships that you have with any of your suggestions. If you can, please suggest appropriate reviewers from several different countries.

Processing the paper through revision

Once the review is returned you will get a notification, and you are asked make a recommendation of action to the chief-editor, who will almost always follow your suggestion.

With aggressive editorial rejections, more of the reviewed papers come back 'Accept' or 'Revision with re-evaluation'. We may still have to reject papers at this stage, because of numbers. If a reviewer is borderline but says 'Accept', we often overrule and say 'Reject'. When doing this, the author needs a reason. ( I have had a reviewer respond 'The article does nothing interesting or unexpected. You can accept it.')

Though the reviewer has suggested acceptance, the editorial board has deemed, after careful consideration of the review and manuscript, that the manuscript is not up to the standards required for publication in the KMJ. Our decision is based on the interest of this article to our editorial board and to our general readership. Due to a very high volume of submissions our acceptance rate is quite low. We have to select the best of the submissions we receive.

If the paper is accepted but English is poor, do not worry about this, the chief editor will bother the authors about this editing. (We have a small budget for English editing, but not enough to edit all papers.)

Tone and Editing Reviews

Many of our authors are students, or less established researchers. Our goal is not to teach, but it cannot hurt to be supportive. Reading weak papers can be frustrating, but we should try to keep this our of any interactions we have with the authors. Reviewers can also be harsh at times. If you are concerned with the tone that a reviewer takes, you can let me know. On occasion I edit a review to soften it, without substantially changing the review.

As mentioned above we get many useless reviews. Worse even, we get many self-serving reviews-- superficial reviews with comments suggesting/requiring that the authors add references to the reviewer's papers. I edit such suggestions out. If you see a suggested review, and feel it is an unnecessary and self-serving on behalf of the reviewer, please mention it to me (there is a space for this on the editorial system.)

Submitting Your own papers

Editors are welcome to submit papers to the KMJ. Lets face it-- our submissions are quite weak, and most anything that you would submit would raise the level of our articles. We would love to have your submissions. Of course, the paper will be assigned to a different editor and will be properly reviewed. But I will try to be a bit more attentive to your paper, and try to get it through the review process faster than usual. This holds also for papers of collegues that you know to be strong. If you let me know that it is an 'invited' paper, then I will try to pay it more attention. For strong articles, we try to give a fast turnover time. (The time of course depends on reviewers, but if a really fast processing is necessary, please make this clear to me, we can sometimes get someone to do a quick job as a favor.)

Of course you will not send your strongest papers to us. Examples of papers that I have submitted to the KMJ are papers that finish a case I missed in another result, or that are fill in rather technical details that I needed to use in a different paper.

If, as editor, you see many submissions that do uninteresting work in an area, we would always welcome you to write a survey that suggests more interesting directions of work in the area.

Other Points

  • Right now our system alerts editors when a review comes back, but doesn't alert editors when an invitation to a reviewer expires without response, or a reviewer doesn't meet their deadline. I hope to change this but for now, it would be nice if editors would look at the system every month or so to see if something has transpired. ( Also, it seems email from our system don't always make it, so checking the system occasionally is a good policy. )
  • On the other hand, we get too many emails when we have invited a new reviewer. You can reduce these by removing your email from the cc in the invitation email, but you cannot get rid of them all (I hope to change this). If emails from KMJ go to spam, please be sure to click any 'this is not spam' button your e-mail program might have.
