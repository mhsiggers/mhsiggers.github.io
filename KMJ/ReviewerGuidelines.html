The Kyungpook Mathematical Journal cannot run without the effort of reviewers. We appreciate a review in any form that you wish to return it, but we have some guidelines that you might like to consider when reviewing your manuscript.

Accepting the task of reviewing an article

Before you accept the task of reviewing an article, please consider.
  • Are you familiar enough with the area to give a helpful review?
  • Are you at arm's length from the authors? ( If not, your review can still be useful, but please disclose any close relationships with the authors.)
  • Can you give a helpful review within the given time period?

What is a helpful review?

The purpose of your review is both to help the editors to decide whether or not to accept the paper for publication, and to help the authors improve the paper whether it is accepted or not. We would ask that you try to include the following.
  • A brief description of what is done in the paper.
  • An assesement of the import, innovation, and interest of the paper to researchers in the area; also of its appropriateness to the KMJ.
  • An assesement of the correctness of the paper.
  • An overall recommendation of: 'Accepted', 'Revision', 'Revision with Re-evaluation', or 'Rejected'.
  • Comments on where you feel the paper might be improved.
We give some advice on making these assesements below. If you are not confident in one of these assesements, you can still give a helpful review. When you return your review on our online system, you will be asked to make a recommendation and give comments to the Author. You will also be able to include 'Confidential Comments to the Editor'. Here you can say that such things as
  • The article appears correct and non-trivial but I am not so familiar with the particular area, so cannot assess how much of an audience it would have.
  • The article is interesting. I have verified the details upto page 12, after this I did not read so closely, but if seems that it should all work.
If you cannot finish an article in the alloted 90 days, but are still willing to review it, please contact the editor. When writing your report please also consider the following points.
  • Tone of Report. Reading math is difficult, and reading poorly written math can be frustrating. This can come through in a report. We are not a top journal, so many of our submissions are from first time or unexperienced authors. We ask that a reviewer think twice, when writing their reports, about whether or not more constructive comments might be appropriate. (An 'uninteresting' result might rather have 'too specific an audience'. An 'easy' result might just be 'not up to the standard of the journal'.)
  • Poor English. Articles submitted to the KMJ often have poor English. If this is the case with the article you are reviewing, please make a comment that the English should be edited by a native speaker, but do not waste too much time going into specifics. Once mathematical issues have been dealt with and the article is accepted, we will bother the authors about having the English fixed. That said, if the English is poor enough that it makes it difficult to understand the paper, a quick response to that effect is not out of line. "It is unclear what the purpose (or main results) of this article are. English must be edited before I can give it a fair review."
  • Plagerism and Similiariy Reports. Sadly, we have had instances where authors submit slightly altered versions of papers they have published elsewhere. Because of this we have started providing reviewers with automated 'similarity reports'. We ask a review to view these with a grain of salt. A high 'similarity score' may mean there a similar paper has been found, but it also might just mean that the authors paper is posted on the ArXiv. If you base a rejection on a similarity report, please be sure to look carefully at it.
  • Too many references. Citation inflation is a problem. It is natural for an author to cite their earlier works, but if there seem to be an inordinate number of references that are not referred to for a specific reason, especially self references, please ask that the author justify or remove the references.
  • Suggesting references. Please be careful about suggesting any specific references, but especially references to your own papers. When suggesting a specific reference, please give a reason ( such as, 'this result is proved in [?]'). Frequently we get comments that a paper lacks motivation or background. One can address this without specific references. If a review seems too self-serving, it will be thrown out, wasting everyone's time and effort.

Making a recommendation

The KMJ looks to publish high quality original research of broad international interest, from all fields of Mathematics.

Original research of broad international interest

The KMJ published articles in all fields of mathematics, so results that are too specific are inappropriate.
  • As a rule of thumb, we are looking to publish articles that would be of interest to more than two or three 'pods' of co-authors, in more than two countries.
  • Accepted articles should have some novelty in their proof method, or have original and interesting results. In particular, articles that simply extend known proofs to more general settings, or present rote calculations in unintesting cases, should not be accepted. Such articles are more appropriate to journals with a more specific scope.

Assessing Quality

The KMJ gets about 400 submissions per year, and publishes about 60. About 100 submissions are sent on to reviewers, so only about 1 or 2 in 3 reviewed manuscripts should be accepted. For reviewers unfamiliar with the KMJ:
  • The KMJ is listed on the ESCI (Emerging Sources Citation Index). You are likely familiar with the SCIE ( Science Citation Index Expanded ) as it is the de-facto journal accreditation in many countries. The ESCI is a stage from which scientific journals are evaluated for inclusion on the SCIE. We hope to soon to be listed on the SCIE, so aim to publish papers that would be accepted at least in the lower journals on this list. We suggest a reviewer make a recommendation as they would for a familiar journal low on the SCIE list.
  • If unsure of what recommendation to make, please recommend 'Revision', be clear about what the strengths and weaknesses are ('well written', 'middling originality', etc..) and then let the editor know that you are unsure whether or not the article is strong enough for the journal.
